About ​Charleston, SC

Charleston is the largest city in South Carolina, serving as the county seat of Charleston County and the central hub of the Charleston metropolitan area. Situated just south of the state's coastal midpoint, Charleston is located on Charleston Harbor, an Atlantic Ocean inlet formed by the confluence of the Ashley, Cooper, and Wando rivers. According to the 2020 census, Charleston's population was 150,227. By 2023, the broader metropolitan area encompassing Berkeley, Charleston, and Dorchester counties was estimated to have 849,417 residents. This makes it the third-largest metropolitan area in South Carolina and the 71st-largest in the United States.

Founded in 1670 as Charles Town in honor of King Charles II, Charleston originally sat at Albemarle Point on the Ashley River's west bank (now Charles Towne Landing). The settlement moved to its current location in 1680 and, within a decade, became the fifth-largest city in North America. It remained unincorporated during the colonial era, governed by a colonial legislature and a governor appointed by Parliament. Election districts were based on Anglican parishes, with social services managed by Anglican wardens and vestries. Charleston adopted its current spelling in 1783 upon incorporation as a city. While the state government relocated to Columbia in 1788 due to population shifts inland, Charleston remained one of the ten largest U.S. cities through the 1840 census.

Charleston's historical significance is deeply tied to its role as a leading port in the American slave trade. Local traders, such as Joseph Wragg, broke the Royal African Company's monopoly, facilitating large-scale slave importation during the 18th century. Nearly half of all enslaved people brought to the United States passed through Charleston. In 2018, the city issued a formal apology for its involvement in the transatlantic slave trade.
